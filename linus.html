CLIENT OVERVIEW

Inspired by French bicycle design of the 50s and 60s, Linus Bikes created bicycles that preserves the elegance and pure form of that era. The founders Adam McDermott and Chad Kushner brought their influence to Venice, CA to give consumers “a simple, elegant bike for riding around and doing stuff.”

THE CHALLENGE

As a company that offers a broad range of bicycles and accessories for both the casual and commuter cyclists, they wanted to offer the ability to customize their own bikes in order to generate more sales and lower their return rate. Additionally, Linus wanted to expand their brand beyond retail by embracing the values of community and ecology. There was a great need to encompass the mindset and lifestyle of their customers.

THE SOLUTION

Our goal was to build a mobile app using Augmented Reality technology to streamline bike customization and increase engagement with the brand through social sharing events and stamping the hashtag #LINUSLIFESTYLE.

UNDERSTANDING OUR USERS

We started by making our way down to the Linus store location in Venice, CA to get an understanding of their customer base. We found that most of the users were practical buyers who enjoyed the vintage style. Most of the purchases were made online with only an average 10% of their sales were made in store.


“This place is more like a showroom now. Everything is done online.” - Linus Store Manager

COMPETITIVE AND COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

We conducted a C & C Analysis to find out what the competitors were doing. The focus was to uncover what features were boosting their users’ experiences. Some of the biggest competitors were Solé, Pure Cycles and Civia and this is what we were able to unearth:

  • Social Sharing was predominate across each platform
  • Product Videos gave a better description of each item
  • Customization was only offered with one competitor
  • Only one large competitor had the use of AR technology

USABILITY TESTING

In the beginning was confident that our design choices were iron clad until we came across several road blocks in our user testing phase. Again, users didn’t know what the AR symbol stood for, so I had to find a way to onboard the process.

“Let’s put italicized text next to the symbol in red.” FAIL

“Maybe I should create a raised button around the symbol to indicate that it needs to be pressed.”FAIL

insert image here

Users would just add each item to the cart and go through the whole checkout process thinking that they would be able to see their customization at the end. Our objective was for them to use the customization feature before adding anything to their cart.

ITERATION

I continued to watch the user flow of each person we tested in order to find a pattern and I discovered that people naturally look for the “Add to Cart” button no matter the task. So to overcome the challenge of our feature being overlooked, I implemented a “Customize Bike” button with the AR symbol as a reference in order to get them accustomed to that visual lingo. Only then the light bulb went up. A common comment was, “Oh wait, I just saw that symbol! So that’s what it means.”

insert image here

PROTOTYPE

Through research and constant user testing, we were able to
RE-DEFINE THE BRAND with a simple solution to provide visually appealing and solicitous events within the app.
And……..
OVERCOME THE RETURN STATISTIC by giving our users the ability to not only customize their bikes, but the option to add their accessories straight to the cart from the AR screen!

CONCLUSION

Although we were able to successfully portray the idea of using AR technology and gained incredible insight from testing and retesting the AR rendering, I would have liked to explore a way to prototype using actual AR software and have access to the users’ camera to display real room placement for their bikes. Overall I really enjoyed working with this client because I was able to learn a new technology that I had very little experience with. I am now completely intrigued with using this technology with more products in the future.
