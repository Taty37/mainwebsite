CLIENT OVERVIEW

Legit Bud Store is an online dispensary that is based in California that delivers medicinal marijuana across the United States, but internationally as well. Their mission is to provide service to everyone who needs it in the easiest way possible.

THE CHALLENGE

For Legit Bud Store to be a global company, sales were not to their expectancy. They wanted to appeal to a larger customer base and give their consumers a smooth checkout process that will excite them to revisit and reuse their delivery service time and time again.

THE SOLUTION

My goal for LBS was to redesign their website in order to increase traffic and sales by researching the cannabis industry, revamping the home page, developing a more intuitive navigation and comfortable and quick checkout process.

LEARNING THE INDUSTRY

Cannabis delivery has hit the California area by storm. In southern Californian cities, “delivery is king”, as Lauren Yoshiko from Forbes magazine would say. Eaze, an online cannabis marketplace and delivery app, facilitates more than 500,000 deliveries alone since the sale of marijuana for recreational purposes began in January. Users want their products fast, in good quality with full discretion.

COMPETITIVE & COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

Many surrounding companies were successfully doing this, so I did a Comparative and Competitive Analysis to see where LBS matched up and this is what I found:

  • All demographics were catered to (novice and advanced smokers)
  • Even though California accepts recreational purchases, a rec card was still required on each website
  • Delivery was limited to local areas
  • Customer feedback was president
  • Standard payment methods included Credit Card and cash

HEURISTIC EVALUATION

After researching the cannabis delivery industry and other competitors, I went back to LBS and did a full scrub of their website. A Heuristic Evaluation brought to my attention that LBS were not following the industry standards. For instance:

  • The site didn’t specify the demographic they were catering to
  • The navigation was not cohesive with its items
  • Support was not active
  • Pay options were not common (CashApp, Walmart & Bitcoin)
  • No Reviews on any of the products
  • No rec/medical marijuana card requirement
  • No Social Media presence

LISTENING TO OUR USERS

From the information I gathered, the biggest challenge was to synthesize and accommodate our users in a way that best fits their needs while keeping the company abreast with the industry standards.

USER FLOW

Even though I did several flows of my own of the website, the goal was to make sure the site was intuitive and seamless for our users. There is where I found further frustrations that needed to be addressed. 9 out 10 of my users exited the website without attempting to go through the checkout process. Only 1 understood what Bitcoin was and how it could be used.

  • “I don’t know where to start.”
  • “340 dollars?! Why is this so expensive?”
  • “Cash App, Walmart and Western Union? Thats kind of sketch.”
  • “I have no way to pay for this… I guess I will just fly back to Seattle and walk down to the store and get my brownies.”

So what's more effective for this site in order to increase traffic, sales and loyalty?

USABILITY TESTING

In the beginning was confident that our design choices were iron clad until we came across several road blocks in our user testing phase. Again, users didn’t know what the AR symbol stood for, so I had to find a way to onboard the process.

“Let’s put italicized text next to the symbol in red.” FAIL

“Maybe I should create a raised button around the symbol to indicate that it needs to be pressed.”FAIL

insert image here

Users would just add each item to the cart and go through the whole checkout process thinking that they would be able to see their customization at the end. Our objective was for them to use the customization feature before adding anything to their cart.

ITERATION

I continued to watch the user flow of each person we tested in order to find a pattern and I discovered that people naturally look for the “Add to Cart” button no matter the task. So to overcome the challenge of our feature being overlooked, I implemented a “Customize Bike” button with the AR symbol as a reference in order to get them accustomed to that visual lingo. Only then the light bulb went up. A common comment was, “Oh wait, I just saw that symbol! So that’s what it means.”

CONCLUSION

Although we were able to successfully portray the idea of using AR technology and gained incredible insight from testing and retesting the AR rendering, I would have liked to explore a way to prototype using actual AR software and have access to the users’ camera to display real room placement for their bikes. Overall I really enjoyed working with this client because I was able to learn a new technology that I had very little experience with. I am now completely intrigued with using this technology with more products in the future.
